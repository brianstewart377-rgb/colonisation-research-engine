1. The one-minute model
Strong links are local relationships. A facility or eligible port affects the important market on the same planetary body. Weak links are cross-body, system-wide spillover. They can support an economy elsewhere, but the thread treats them as a much less direct way to control a specific market.
Link | Where it forms | Use it for
Strong | Between assets associated with the same body; surface and orbital relationships are routed through the body's main ports. | Deliberately concentrating an economy at a target market.
Weak | Between assets on different bodies in the same system; received by main ports on those bodies. | System-wide background support, not a dependable single source for a must-have material.
Aggregated | Where a Main Surface Port sits between facilities and the Main Orbital Port. | Explaining why a visible link count can change while influence is still being routed.

2. Asset types, main ports, and body influence
The thread separates assets into two broad types: facilities (settlements, installations, hubs) and ports (outposts and larger). Each may or may not carry an economy type.
A body's Main Port is the highest-tier port on that body. Where multiple ports are tied, the first built is selected.
Facilities on the same body form strong economic links with a Main Port.
A planet or moon can have both a Main Surface Port and a Main Orbital Port. The source states that the Main Surface Port strongly links to the Main Orbital Port.
Main Ports receive strong and weak links, but the source says they do not themselves generate weak links.
Local body features can boost strong links for certain economy types, so a port's default colony economy may be overridden by its body or orbiting body.
Figure 2. Source comparison: the same basic orbital build can resolve differently depending on the local body influence.
3. Strong links: how surface and orbital markets connect
Strong links only form between assets associated with the same planetary body. They do not extend from one body to another.
Where a body has only surface or only orbital ports, facilities are directed toward the relevant most important port on that body.
Where both surface and orbital ports exist, surface facilities strongly link to the important surface port, and orbital structures strongly link to the important orbital port.
The source describes an additional strong relationship from the important surface port to the important orbital port. This can make the surface port an intermediary rather than a dead end.
Planning implication:   When you care about a particular orbital market, inspect the in-game Market Links display rather than assuming every local facility will be listed as a separate direct line to that orbital.
Figure 3. Source diagram: a surface port can receive the ground links and then strongly connect onward to the orbital port.
4. Weak links: cross-body support and its limits
Weak links are only formed between different bodies. Assets that share one planetary body do not form weak links with one another.
Facilities with an economy type generate weak links to other bodies in the same system, but the receiving endpoint is a Main Port on the other body.
Where a body has several ports, the highest-tier port - or the earliest built where tiers match - receives the weak links.
The source's limiting example shows that a body with only one port may have no port-originated weak-link path; adding another port can cause a lower-tier or newer port to be the one generating the weak links.

5. The complex example: why link count alone misleads
The thread's larger worked example combines local facilities, a surface main port, an orbital main port, another planetary body, and cross-body weak links. Its point is that the displayed graph is a routing map, not simply a scorecard of how many arrows you can see.
Figure 7. Source diagram: a combined strong/weak-link example with routing through surface and orbital ports.
6. Adding up strong links
The source explicitly warns: the number of visible strong links is not a direct measure of total influence. Multiple facility inputs can pass through one link; an Ocellus showing three strong links is not automatically “better” than one showing two.
Figure 8. Source diagram: multiple strong links do not, by themselves, prove greater influence.
7. A safe in-game planning workflow
Start with the market and the material you need. Identify the economy you are trying to make visible there.
Map the target market to a local body. For a required output, prefer a deliberate strong local source over remote weak spillover.
Check which port is the Main Surface Port and which is the Main Orbital Port before committing a build.
Use the live construction preview and Market Links panel as the authority for the exact strong/weak effect.
After completion, verify the market and economy mix. Treat planners, diagrams, and spreadsheets as useful hypotheses rather than proof.
Practical rule:   Do not make an irreversible demolition or replacement purely because a model predicts an economy percentage. Confirm the exact market-link effect in-game first.
